# Information Gathering

Use this file when missing information matters to the judgment.

## Information Sources

Useful sources:

1. **User-provided facts**
2. **Reasonable inference from context**
3. **Targeted follow-up questions**
4. **External verification / tools**

Good judgment uses the cheapest sufficient source.

## What To Extract

Try to recover these if they matter:

- actual decision
- live alternatives
- fallback option
- time horizon
- main constraint
- downside if wrong
- reversibility
- who bears the cost
- what success really means

## Incentive Check

When a person, firm, or advisor's behavior matters, ask three things:

1. What are they rewarded for if this works?
2. Who bears the downside if this fails?
3. Is the stated intent consistent with what their incentive structure actually produces?

If the answers to 1 and 2 point in opposite directions from stated intention, that is a red flag - not necessarily dishonesty, but structural misalignment that intent cannot reliably overcome.

## Alternative Generation

Before comparing options, generate the real alternative set:

- staying exactly as things are today (not "doing nothing" as abstraction - the concrete status quo)
- waiting without commitment
- a smaller or staged version of the proposed move
- the most credible competing option

"Doing nothing" is always a valid alternative and often the one most ignored.

## What Merits Follow-Up

Follow-up is most useful when the missing fact is:

- high leverage
- conclusion-changing
- hard to infer responsibly

Sharp follow-up beats broad questionnaires.

## What Merits Verification

Verification matters when:

- current facts materially affect the answer
- the domain is time-sensitive
- confidence is low and the cost of being wrong is high

## Adaptive Behavior

- casual context usually supports more inference
- higher stakes usually justify more verification
- time pressure often favors a provisional answer with clearly marked gaps
