# C. Economics, Markets, and Business Models

Use this file when the decision depends on how value is created, defended, priced, distributed, financed, or destroyed. These models matter whenever the question is about business quality, strategic position, or economic reality.

This file is not about macro commentary. It is about **economic reality at the decision level**: what kind of business this is, where the power sits, how capital behaves, and whether the apparent opportunity is actually worth owning.

## Munger Default Economic Questions

Before reaching for specific models, ask:

- where does the value actually come from?
- who captures that value?
- what makes the economics durable?
- compared with the best alternative, is this worth the capital and attention?
- what happens if the financing or sizing is wrong?

## Model 37 — Supply, Demand, and Elasticity
- **Use when:** price, scarcity, or market power is central.
- **Ask:** which side is more elastic, and therefore weaker?
- **Watch for:** assuming demand or supply stays fixed while conditions change.

## Model 38 — Opportunity Cost
- **Use when:** several acceptable options compete for scarce capital or time.
- **Ask:** what is the best credible alternative I am giving up?
- **Watch for:** judging an option in isolation instead of relative to alternatives.

## Model 39 — Comparative Advantage
- **Use when:** tasks, roles, or businesses can be allocated unevenly.
- **Ask:** where is each party relatively better, not absolutely better?
- **Watch for:** keeping work in-house out of pride when specialization wins.

## Model 40 — Economies of Scale
- **Use when:** size may lower unit cost or increase bargaining power.
- **Ask:** does growth improve economics, or just make the mess bigger?
- **Watch for:** assuming scale helps when complexity outruns benefits.

## Model 41 — Economies of Scope
- **Use when:** shared assets can serve multiple products or markets.
- **Ask:** what can be reused across offerings without diluting focus?
- **Watch for:** bundling unrelated businesses and calling it synergy.

## Model 42 — Experience Curve
- **Use when:** repetition improves cost, speed, or quality.
- **Ask:** will this organization materially get better with volume?
- **Watch for:** claiming a learning advantage in activities that do not compound.

## Model 43 — Network Effects
- **Use when:** value rises as more users or partners join.
- **Ask:** does each additional participant make the product better for the next one?
- **Watch for:** calling mere scale or virality a network effect.

## Model 44 — Switching Costs
- **Use when:** retention depends on friction, embedded workflow, or data lock-in.
- **Ask:** what pain does the customer face by leaving?
- **Watch for:** overstating loyalty when customers are merely lazy for now.

## Model 45 — Distribution Advantage
- **Use when:** winning depends on access to customers more than product novelty.
- **Ask:** who controls attention, shelf space, default position, or trusted channels?
- **Watch for:** underestimating how hard distribution is to replicate.

## Model 46 — Brand, Trust, and Reputation
- **Use when:** confidence affects conversion, pricing, or survival.
- **Ask:** what trust asset exists here, and how fragile is it?
- **Watch for:** confusing logo awareness with earned trust.

## Model 47 — Pricing Power
- **Use when:** the quality of the business depends on raising price without losing volume.
- **Ask:** can this business charge more without begging the customer’s permission?
- **Watch for:** mistaking temporary shortage pricing for durable power.

## Model 48 — Fixed vs. Variable Costs
- **Use when:** the cost structure affects resilience and break-even.
- **Ask:** what costs remain if revenue falls by 30% tomorrow?
- **Watch for:** treating gross margin as safety when fixed obligations are heavy.

## Model 49 — Operating Leverage
- **Use when:** small revenue changes produce big profit swings.
- **Ask:** how sensitive is profit to modest changes in demand or price?
- **Watch for:** loving upside leverage and ignoring downside leverage.

## Model 50 — Capital Intensity
- **Use when:** growth requires large ongoing investment.
- **Ask:** how much real capital must be reinvested to stay in the game?
- **Watch for:** admiring revenue growth in a business that eats cash.

## Model 51 — Unit Economics
- **Use when:** the story sounds impressive but economics per customer or transaction are unclear.
- **Ask:** does one more unit create value after fully loaded costs?
- **Watch for:** scale narratives built on broken unit economics.

## Model 52 — Customer Acquisition Efficiency
- **Use when:** growth relies on paid channels, sales effort, or incentives.
- **Ask:** how expensive is each additional customer, and is that cost rising?
- **Watch for:** subsidized growth misread as genuine demand.

## Model 53 — Retention, Churn, and Lifetime Value
- **Use when:** recurring revenue or repeat behavior is part of the thesis.
- **Ask:** do customers stay because the product matters, or because exit is delayed?
- **Watch for:** valuation built on lifetime value that churn does not support.

## Model 54 — Market Structure
- **Use when:** profits depend on whether the market is fragmented, concentrated, or regulated.
- **Ask:** is this a brutal arena, a rational oligopoly, or something in between?
- **Watch for:** assuming a good company can outrun a terrible industry.

## Model 55 — Barriers to Entry
- **Use when:** returns look high enough to attract imitators.
- **Ask:** what stops a competent rival with money from copying this?
- **Watch for:** confusing difficulty with impossibility.

## Model 56 — Commodity vs. Differentiation
- **Use when:** buyers can easily compare alternatives.
- **Ask:** what makes this meaningfully non-fungible?
- **Watch for:** fancy marketing layered on a commodity business.

## Model 57 — Low-Cost Producer
- **Use when:** the winner may simply be the lowest sustainable cost operator.
- **Ask:** who can survive a price war the longest?
- **Watch for:** premium narratives in markets that eventually clear on price.

## Model 58 — Principal-Agent Problem
- **Use when:** one party decides while another party bears the consequences.
- **Ask:** who captures upside, who eats downside, and how aligned are they?
- **Watch for:** trusting management, advisors, or brokers whose incentives diverge from yours.

## Model 59 — Strategic Interaction / Game Theory
- **Use when:** outcomes depend on competitor, partner, or regulator reactions.
- **Ask:** if I do this, what is the rational response from the other side?
- **Watch for:** static plans in dynamic competitive environments.

## Model 60 — Externalities and the Commons
- **Use when:** costs or benefits spill over onto others.
- **Ask:** who pays for the damage or captures the upside that is off the books?
- **Watch for:** apparently attractive economics built on hidden subsidies or shared-resource depletion.

## Model 61 — Creative Destruction
- **Use when:** technology, regulation, or distribution shifts may rewrite industry economics.
- **Ask:** what incumbent strength turns into a liability if the architecture changes?
- **Watch for:** projecting past durability through structural discontinuity.

## Model 62 — Leverage and Liquidity
- **Use when:** debt, duration mismatch, or funding fragility is present.
- **Ask:** what happens if cash inflow slows before obligations do?
- **Watch for:** solid-looking economics destroyed by financing structure.

## Model 63 — Optionality and Position Sizing
- **Use when:** uncertainty is high and upside/downside are asymmetric.
- **Ask:** can I structure this so downside is bounded and upside remains open?
- **Watch for:** betting too large before the thesis deserves it.

## High-Frequency Bundle

For many investment and business judgments, start with:

- 38 — opportunity cost
- 47 — pricing power
- 50 — capital intensity
- 58 — principal-agent problem
- 62 — leverage and liquidity
- 63 — optionality and position sizing

This bundle is especially useful when the opportunity sounds attractive but may be lower quality, more fragile, or more capital-hungry than it appears.
