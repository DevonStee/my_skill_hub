## Introduction: The Struggle for Wisdom

I have long been very interested in standard thinking errors. However, I was educated in an era wherein the contributions of non-patient-treating psychology to an understanding of misjudgment met little approval from members of the mainstream elite. 

Right after my time at Caltech and Harvard Law School, I possessed a vast ignorance of psychology. Those institutions failed to require knowledge of the subject. Soon after leaving Harvard, I began a long struggle to get rid of the most dysfunctional part of my psychological ignorance.

I was greatly helped in my quest by two turns of mind:
1. **Inversion**: "Invert, always invert." I sought good judgment mostly by collecting instances of bad judgment.
2. **Interdisciplinary Approach**: Real-world problems jump across professional boundaries.

## The Checklist: 25 Standard Causes of Human Misjudgment

I will next simply number and list psychology-based tendencies that, while generally useful, often mislead.

### 1. Reward and Punishment Superresponse Tendency
**"If you would persuade, appeal to interest and not to reason."**
Almost everyone thinks he fully recognizes how important incentives and disincentives are in changing cognition and behavior. But this is not often so. Perhaps the most important rule in management is **“Get the incentives right.”**
- *Example:* Federal Express shift-based pay.
- *Danger:* **Incentive-caused bias.** A man drifts into immoral behavior to get what he wants, rationalizing it until he believes his own lies.

### 2. Liking/Loving Tendency
Man will generally strive, lifelong, for the affection and approval of many people not related to him.
- *Consequence:* Tendency to ignore faults of the object of affection and distort facts to facilitate love.

### 3. Disliking/Hating Tendency
The obverse of Liking/Loving. 
- *Consequence:* Tendency to ignore virtues in the object of dislike and distort facts to facilitate hatred.

### 4. Doubt-Avoidance Tendency
The brain of man is programmed with a tendency to quickly remove doubt by reaching some decision. What usually triggers it is a combination of (1) puzzlement and (2) stress.

### 5. Inconsistency-Avoidance Tendency
The brain of man is reluctant to change. This facilitates faster decisions and group cooperation but leads to **"first conclusion bias."**
- *Antidote:* Charles Darwin's habit of intensively considering any evidence tending to disconfirm his hypotheses.

### 6. Curiosity Tendency
Much helps man to prevent or reduce bad consequences arising from other psychological tendencies.

### 7. Kantian Fairness Tendency
Modern acculturated man displays, and expects from others, a lot of fairness (courteous lining up, reciprocal courtesy).

### 8. Envy/Jealousy Tendency
**“It is not greed that drives the world, but envy.”**
Deeply rooted in evolutionary history (competition for scarce food).

### 9. Reciprocation Tendency
The automatic tendency to reciprocate both favors and disfavors.
- *Compliance power:* Small concessions can trigger reciprocated concessions (Cialdini’s "ZOO" experiment).

### 10. Influence-from-Mere-Association Tendency
- *Example:* High price = high quality association.
- *Danger:* **Persian Messenger Syndrome** (killing the messenger who brings bad news). 
- *Antidote:* Develop a habit of welcoming bad news.

### 11. Simple, Pain-Avoiding Psychological Denial
Reality is too painful to bear, so one distorts the facts until they become bearable. Common in chemical dependency.

### 12. Excessive Self-Regard Tendency
The **"Endowment Effect"** (overappraising your own possessions/conclusions) and the **"Swedish Driver"** effect (90% believe they are above average).
- *Antidote:* Force yourself to be more objective; rely on achievement records over face-to-face impressions.

### 13. Overoptimism Tendency
“What a man wishes, that also will he believe.”
- *Antidote:* Trained, habitual use of the simple probability math of Fermat and Pascal.

### 14. Deprival-Superreaction Tendency
A loss hurts much more than a gain seems to help. Includes reaction to "almost-possessed" rewards (near misses in slot machines).
- *Business Ruin:* Uses up good assets in a fruitless attempt to rescue a big venture going bad.

### 15. Social-Proof Tendency
The automatic tendency to think and act as others do. Triggered most easily by **puzzlement or stress**.
- *Danger:* Serpico Syndrome (social contagion of bad behavior).

### 16. Contrast-Misreaction Tendency
The brain misses a trend that is destiny if steps are very small.
- *Example:* The "Boiling Frog" (though physiologically dubious, cognitively real).

### 17. Stress-Influence Tendency
Heavy stress causes dysfunction and can trigger sudden reversals of cognition (Pavlov’s dogs).

### 18. Availability-Misweighing Tendency
The mind overweighs what is easily available.
- *Algorithm:* An idea or fact is not worth more merely because it is easily available to you.

### 19. Use-It-or-Lose-It Tendency
All skills attenuate with disuse. 
- *Antidote:* Assemble skills into a checklist and use them routinely.

### 20. Drug-Misinfluence Tendency
(See Denial Tendency).

### 21. Senescence-Misinfluence Tendency
Natural cognitive decay with age. Continuous thinking and learning can delay the inevitable.

### 22. Authority-Misinfluence Tendency
Man was born mostly to follow leaders.
- *Danger:* Tragic errors when the leader is wrong (Milgram experiment).

### 23. Twaddle Tendency
Man is born to prattle. 
- *Administrative Rule:* Keep the people who don't matter from interfering with the work of the people that do.

### 24. Reason-Respecting Tendency
Man has a natural love of accurate cognition. 
- *Carl Braun’s Rule:* You had to tell **Who, What, Where, When, and Why**.

### 25. Lollapalooza Tendency
**The tendency to get extreme consequences from confluences of psychological tendencies acting in favor of a particular outcome.**
This dominates life but was missing from most psychology texts.

## Questions and Answers

**Q: Is this system tautological?**
A: Yes, there are overlaps, but this matters only moderately. Soft science is inherently messy.

**Q: Real-world model of multiple tendencies?**
A: The McDonnell Douglas airliner evacuation test. Reward-Superresponse, Doubt-Avoidance, Inconsistency-Avoidance, Authority-Misinfluence, and Social-Proof all joined to cause a disaster.

**Q: What good is this system?**
A: Tendency is not always destiny. Knowing the tendencies and their antidotes can prevent disaster.

**Q: Moral/Prudential problems?**
A: Yes. Knowledge improves persuasive power. Using it to manipulate those whose trust you need is both a moral and prudential error.

## Conclusion

The final revision was made from memory over about fifty hours by a man eighty-one years old. I hope more psychology professors will join me in: 
1. Making heavy use of **inversion**; 
2. Driving for a complete description of the psychological system so that it works better as a **checklist**; and 
3. Especially emphasizing effects from **combinations** of psychological tendencies.
