# The Pursuit of Worldly Wisdom

**Charlie Munger**, the billionaire business partner of Warren Buffett, is not only one of the best investors the world has witnessed, but he’s also one of the best thinkers. Munger has illuminated timeless wisdom in the minds’ search algorithm, Academic Economics, stretch goals, mental models, the value of thinking backward and forward, problem-solving, partnerships, and inversion among others.

> "What is elementary, worldly wisdom? Well, the first rule is that you can't really know anything if you just remember isolated facts and try and bang ‘em back. **If the facts don't hang together on a latticework of theory, you don't have them in a usable form.**" — *Charlie Munger*

---

## The Path to Worldly Wisdom

In his book, *Charlie Munger: The Complete Investor*, Tren Griffin lays out Munger’s path to worldly wisdom. Munger believes that by using a range of different models from many different disciplines—psychology, history, mathematics, physics, philosophy, biology, and so on—a person can use the combined output of the synthesis to produce something that has more value than the sum of its parts.

Robert Hagstrom, in *Investing: The Last Liberal Art*, states: 
> “Each discipline entwines with, and in the process strengthens, every other. From each discipline the thoughtful person draws significant mental models, the key ideas that combine to produce a cohesive understanding. Those who cultivate this broad view are well on their way to achieving worldly wisdom.”

### The Pleasure of Learning
Munger actually has fun when he is learning. Many people find investing a chore compared to gambling, which generates dopamine despite negative net present value. Munger has created a system—worldly wisdom—that generates the same chemical rewards in an activity that has a **positive net present value**. When you learn something new, your brain gives itself a chemical reward, motivating the work necessary to be a successful investor.

## Latticework of Mental Models

Munger used a **latticework of mental models** in developing his approach. Herbert Simon, in his autobiography *Models of My Life*, captured the idea:

> “A large part of the difference between the experienced decision maker and the novice... is that he had at his disposal repertoires of possible actions; that he had checklists of things to think about before he acted; and that he had mechanisms in his mind to evoke these.”

The latticework model conveys **interconnectedness**. We need a working knowledge of all segments and how they link. As the Japanese proverb says: *“The frog in a well knows nothing of the mighty ocean.”*

### Example: The Price-Demand Paradox
To illustrate the method, Munger gave the example of a business that raises the price of its product and yet sells more. 
- **Economics:** Might initially see a violation of supply and demand.
- **Psychology:** Might conclude it's a **Giffen good**, or that high price signals high quality.
- **Incentives:** Alternatively, the seller might have bribed the purchasing agents (e.g., raising mutual fund commissions to bribe brokers to betray their clients).

## Worldly Wisdom vs. Precise Wrongness

In Munger’s view, it is **better to be worldly wise than to spend lots of time working with a single model that is precisely wrong.** A multiple-model approach that is only approximately right will produce a far better outcome in human or social systems.

### The Fox and the Hedgehog
Munger is what the poet Archilochus calls a **fox**: *“The fox knows many things; the hedgehog one great thing.”* 

> “The theory of modern education is that you need a general education before you specialize. And I think to some extent, before you’re going to be a great stock picker, you need some general education.” — *Charlie Munger*

Former Microsoft CEO Bill Gates said: “(he) is truly the broadest thinker I have ever encountered.” Buffett added: “He sees the essence of everything before you even finish the sentence.”

## The Discipline of Thinking and Reading

Munger’s breadth of knowledge is intentionally cultivated. Both Munger and Buffett set aside plenty of time each day to **just think**. 
- **The Netflix Trap:** Most people chose electric shocks over spending time alone with their own thoughts (2014 study).
- **Reading:** Munger has read hundreds of biographies. Buffett notes: “You could hardly find a partnership in which two people settle on reading more hours of the day than in ours.”

> “I believe in the discipline of **mastering the best that other people have ever figured out.** I don’t believe in just sitting down and trying to dream it all up yourself. Nobody’s that smart.” — *Charlie Munger*

---

## A Multidisciplinary Approach

Someone who is really smart but devoted to a narrow area is **dangerous to themselves and others**. 
- *Examples:* Macroeconomists disastrous at personal investing; marketing experts who think marketing solves all business problems.

### The 80/90 Rule
> “You may say, ‘My God, this is already getting way too tough.’ But, fortunately, it isn’t that tough—because **eighty or ninety important models will carry about 90 percent of the freight** in making you a worldly wise person. And, of those, only a mere handful really carry very heavy freight.” — *Charlie Munger*

Munger identifies many models in psychology but has never prepared a complete list covering all disciplines. He believes the deep structure of the human mind requires learning it all to **fluency**.

### Learning Machines
> “I constantly see people rise in life who are not the smartest, sometimes not even the most diligent, but they are **learning machines**. They go to bed every night a little wiser than they were when they got up.” — *Charlie Munger*

---

## Learning From Mistakes

Munger likes people admitting they were "complete stupid horses’ asses." 
> “I know I’ll perform better if I **rub my nose in my mistakes.** This is a wonderful trick to learn.” — *Charlie Munger*

- **Simplicity:** One great way to avoid mistakes is to own a business that is simple to understand. “Where you have complexity, by nature you can have fraud and mistakes.”
- **Failure Analysis:** If you cannot explain why you failed, the business was too complex for you. If you cannot determine what you did wrong, you cannot learn.

> “Forgetting your mistakes is a terrible error if you’re trying to improve your cognition. Reality doesn’t remind you. Why not celebrate stupidities in both categories?” — *Charlie Munger*

---

## Conclusion

Munger chosen the word **wisdom** purposefully because mere knowledge from one domain is not enough. To be wise, one must also have experience, common sense, and good judgment. It is about how one actually applies these things across boundaries.
