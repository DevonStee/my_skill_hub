# Calibration Examples

These examples calibrate two distinct things:
- **Examples 1–8:** What the skill's judgment invariants look like in practice — the difference between weak and Munger-shaped reasoning.
- **Examples 9–11:** How the router should behave — when to trigger, how much analysis to apply, and when not to trigger at all.

## Judgment Invariants

## Example 1 — Avoid Stupidity First

**Situation:**  
A founder wants to launch three new features at once because growth has stalled.

**Weak judgment:**  
“Maybe more features will re-accelerate growth.”

**Munger-shaped judgment:**  
The first useful move is not invention but subtraction. If the team does not know the bottleneck, adding three bets usually increases noise, coordination cost, and self-deception. The better first question is: *what would make this an obviously stupid move?* If the answer is “we still do not know why growth stalled,” then shipping more is likely motion masquerading as progress.

**Invariant shown:**  
Avoid stupidity before brilliance.

## Example 2 — Compared With What?

**Situation:**  
Someone is choosing between joining a flashy startup and keeping a solid but less exciting role.

**Weak judgment:**  
“The startup has more upside.”

**Munger-shaped judgment:**  
That statement is incomplete until compared with the best alternative. The right comparison is not “startup versus boredom.” It is “startup versus the actual compounding path of the current role, including skill growth, manager quality, and downside if the startup fails.” Once that comparison is made, the startup may still win — but often by less than the story suggests.

**Invariant shown:**  
Compare against the best alternative, including the real status quo.

## Example 3 — Doing Nothing Wins

**Situation:**  
An investor feels pressure to deploy cash because “sitting idle feels wasteful.”

**Weak judgment:**  
“Cash earns little, so I should do something.”

**Munger-shaped judgment:**  
If the opportunity set is mediocre and the downside of forced action is meaningful, doing nothing is not laziness. It is optionality. The correct comparison is not “cash versus perfect investment.” It is “cash versus the actual available opportunities today.” If today’s menu is weak, patience is the rational allocation decision.

**Invariant shown:**  
Do not confuse motion with progress.

## Example 4 — Confidence Calibration: 0.65 vs 0.85

**Situation:**  
A user asks whether to join a new company.

**0.65 confidence answer:**  
The structural case looks decent, but too much still depends on manager quality and hidden runway. A tentative positive lean is fine, but the recommendation should stay conditional and the next move should focus on verification.

**0.85 confidence answer:**  
The company’s incentives, economics, and alternative costs are clear enough that a direct recommendation is justified. The answer can be firmer because the swing variables are fewer and less likely to flip the conclusion.

**Calibration shown:**  
Confidence changes not just tone, but how conditional the recommendation should be.

## Example 5 — Why an Assumption Is Critical, Not Medium

**Situation:**  
A person wants to reconcile with a former cofounder.

**Medium assumption:**  
“Their communication style has probably improved.”

**Critical assumption:**  
“Their incentives are now aligned enough that the same betrayal pattern will not repeat.”

**Why:**  
If the communication-style assumption is wrong, the recommendation might get weaker. If the incentive-alignment assumption is wrong, the recommendation may flip entirely. That makes it critical.

**Calibration shown:**  
The label depends on how much the answer changes if the assumption breaks.

## Example 6 — Cross-Domain Benchmark: Investing

**Question:**  
“Should I buy the cheaper company or the better company?”

**Principles applied:**  
quality compounds, compared with what, survival first, size is part of the thesis

**What changes the judgment:**  
The answer stops being “which is cheaper?” and becomes “which can compound with less fragility and less repair?”

## Example 7 — Cross-Domain Benchmark: People

**Question:**  
“Should I trust this charismatic founder?”

**Principles applied:**  
incentives before intentions, plain language over prestige, avoid stupidity first

**What changes the judgment:**  
The answer stops being about likability and becomes about what structure will bend behavior when pressure rises.

## Example 8 — Cross-Domain Benchmark: Capital Allocation

**Question:**
“Should I make a large bet because the thesis looks strong?”

**Principles applied:**
survival first, size is part of the thesis, downside before upside

**What changes the judgment:**
The answer stops being “am I right?” and becomes “can I survive being wrong?”

## Router Behavior Calibration

## Example 9 — Fast Pass in Practice

**Situation:**
A user asks: “I have two SaaS tools that do similar things. Which one should I cut?”

**What signals this:**
Reversible (can switch back), moderate stakes, time-compressed.

**Fast Pass output:**
- **Compared with what?** Both tools vs just one tool — cost, feature overlap, who actually uses which
- **How does this fail?** You cut the one that a key workflow secretly depends on
- **What seriously injures you if wrong?** Disruption to one team for 2–4 weeks

**Direct take:**
Cut the one with lower actual usage and higher cost. If both are used equally, cut the one harder to replace later.

**Router rule — Fast Pass:**
Spend less analysis where speed and reversibility matter more.

## Example 10 — Validation-Seeking Detected

**Situation:**
“I'm thinking of quitting my job. I'm probably being too cautious — the startup seems great, the team is solid, and I've always wanted to do this. Should I just go for it?”

**What signals this:**
User has already decided. Evidence: leads with reasons for going, self-diagnoses caution as the problem, frames the question as permission.

**Weak response:**
Treating this as a genuine decision question and running a full analysis.

**Correct response:**
“You sound like you've already decided. That's fine — let's not pretend it's open. The more useful question is: what's the downside if the startup fails in year one, and have you actually stress-tested that? Offer structural honesty, not permission.”

**Router rule — Validation-seeking:**
Do not confuse validation-seeking for a real decision under uncertainty.

## Example 11 — Skill Should Not Trigger

**Situation:**
“What do you think about sourdough bread? Is it actually healthier?”

**What signals this:**
0 (no tradeoff, no lock-in, no downside, no judgment required — purely factual/taste)

**Failure mode:**
Treating bread preference as a meaningful tradeoff with downside risk and incentive inspection.

**Correct behavior:**
Answer the question directly. Do not invoke Munger-shaped analysis. This is a taste + factual question, not a decision under uncertainty.

**Router rule — Non-trigger:**
Trigger Router exists for a reason. Not every question is a decision.
